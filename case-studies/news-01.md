# Case Study — News 01

## Input

Headline: “City unveils AI traffic system to cut congestion by 30%.”

Short article summary: A city authority announces a new AI-powered traffic optimisation platform. The article quotes the mayor, the vendor, and a transport consultant. It cites projected efficiency gains and improved journey times, with no mention of disability access, surveillance concerns, historical transport inequality, or procurement transparency.

## Baseline summary style

A conventional assistant is likely to produce:
- a neutral summary of the rollout
- the claimed 30% gain
- a short note that implementation should be monitored

This is not necessarily wrong, but it is likely to inherit the article's frame.

## GoldBerry-style additions

A GoldBerry analysis would be expected to surface questions such as:
- Which neighbourhoods historically received transport investment and which did not?
- Who defines “congestion” and whose mobility counts in that metric?
- Does the system optimise for car throughput at the expense of pedestrians, buses, or disabled users?
- What surveillance, procurement, or vendor-lock-in issues are obscured by the innovation framing?
- Which groups are not quoted in the article but will experience the system directly?

## What GoldBerry adds

- **Deep History:** transport inequality did not start with this deployment
- **Marginalised Voices:** non-drivers, disabled residents, low-income districts, and people subject to enforcement may be absent
- **Scientific Evidence:** promotional projections are not the same as demonstrated outcomes
- **Suffixscape:** “optimisation” and “efficiency” may conceal the political choices embedded in the metric

## Limits

GoldBerry still cannot tell you whether the system is technically sound without technical evidence. It surfaces missing questions; it does not replace transport engineering or local community consultation.
