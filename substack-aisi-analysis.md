# Who Defines "AI Safety"? The UK's AI Security Institute Through Seven Lenses

### An epistemic audit of the body that audits AI — and what its public framing structurally excludes

---

Last week we ran GoldBerry on the BBC homepage and scored it 2.5 out of 10 for epistemic completeness. The response was immediate: people understood what was missing from the most trusted news brand in English, and they could see the framework working.

Today we turned the seven lenses on something harder: the UK AI Security Institute — the government's own body for advanced AI safety research.

AISI scored 3.5 out of 10.

---

## What AISI says about itself

The AI Security Institute is a £66m/year research organisation within the Department for Science, Innovation and Technology. It has 100+ technical staff, alumni from OpenAI, Google DeepMind, and Oxford, priority access to over £1.5 billion in compute, and pre-deployment access to leading AI models.

Its stated mission: "Equipping governments with a scientific understanding of the risks posed by advanced AI."

Its Chief Technology Officer, who also serves as the Prime Minister's AI Advisor, previously led the Governance team at OpenAI. Its Chief Scientist led teams at OpenAI and Google DeepMind. Its former director came from GCHQ.

Its self-description: "a startup in the government."

---

## What the seven lenses found

**Before we begin: scope.** This analysis audits AISI's public-facing website text — the homepage and About page. It does not claim to assess internal research practice or unpublished stakeholder engagement. The epistemic profile identified here is of the public frame, not necessarily the institution in its entirety.

That caveat matters. But so does this: the public frame IS what shapes external understanding of what AI risk means, who defines it, and for whom. If the public framing is narrow, the public understanding will be narrow — regardless of what happens internally.

### Deep History — absent

AISI presents AI safety as a contemporary technical challenge with no historical depth. There is no reference to the history of technological governance — nuclear weapons regulation, pharmaceutical safety, environmental protection — or to what those histories teach about regulatory capture, the limitations of self-regulation by creators, and the decades-long timescales of institutional learning.

The movement of personnel between AI companies and their regulator has historical precedents in finance, pharma, and energy. These precedents — and their lessons — are not referenced.

### Cross-Cultural — absent

AISI's framing is entirely Anglophone and Western. The leadership pipeline is OpenAI → DeepMind → Oxford → GCHQ. There is no reference to how China, India, Brazil, Nigeria, or the African Union think about AI risk. No mention of the EU AI Act, UNESCO's AI Ethics Recommendation, or any non-Anglophone governance framework.

AI safety as defined on aisi.gov.uk is the view from London, San Francisco, and Oxford.

### Marginalised Voices — the central absence

Zero references to people affected by AI deployment. Zero references to civil society organisations, trade unions, disability communities, racial justice organisations, or Global South perspectives.

The word "public" appears once — as the object of "keep the public safe." The public does not speak, advise, participate, or co-design. Safety is defined for people, not with people.

## What the grammar is doing

GoldBerry includes the Suffixscape — a linguistic diagnostic layer that catches the grammatical patterns institutions use to claim action while structurally preventing it.

**"Governments have a critical role to play in ensuring advanced AI is safe, secure and beneficial"**
"Safe, secure and beneficial" — three adjectives doing enormous epistemic work. Safe for whom? Secure against what threats, defined by whom? Beneficial by whose measure? Each word claims completeness without specifying a framework.

**"Building the world's leading understanding of advanced AI risks and solutions"**
"World's leading" is an unauditable claim presented as fact. Leading by what metric? Assessed by whom?

**"We designed AISI like a startup in the government"**
"Startup" imports Silicon Valley organisational mythology into a democratic institution. It claims agility and disruption — values in tension with democratic accountability and public consultation.

**"Keep the public safe"**
The public is the object, not the subject. AISI keeps; the public is kept. The grammar removes public agency from the safety equation.

---

## The synthesis

AISI's public framing is:

- **Epistemically narrow** — safety defined within one disciplinary frame
- **Culturally insular** — entirely Anglophone, entirely from the technical-policy pipeline
- **Historically rootless** — no engagement with the history of technology regulation
- **Democratically hollow** — the public is an object, never a subject
- **Experientially absent** — no testimony, no narrative, no sense of what AI harm feels like
- **Future-modelling restricted** — models what AI can do, not what AI does to people

This is not a criticism of the technical research, which may be excellent. It is a diagnosis of the epistemic frame within which that research is presented to the world.

---

## What could change

The solutions are not expensive:

**Establish a public voice.** Create a public advisory panel of affected communities — workers, disability advocates, racial justice organisations, Global South researchers. Structural role, not consultation exercise.

**Expand the disciplinary frame.** The £15m+ grant fund should include social science alongside computer science. Ethnography of deployment effects. Historical analysis of technology governance.

**Historicise the safety claim.** "What did we learn from nuclear regulation, pharmaceutical safety, and financial regulation about industry-regulator proximity?" AISI has the credibility to ask this question proactively. It should.

## The pattern

Two analyses now. The BBC scored 2.5. AISI scored 3.5.

The BBC is a news organisation — speed and volume are its operating logic. AISI is a research body — depth and rigour are its stated values. AISI has more resources, more time, and an explicit mandate to understand AI risk. It should score higher. It does — but only by one point.

The pattern across both analyses: what's missing is not random. It's structural. The same five lenses are absent in both — Indigenous Knowledge, Deep History, Cross-Cultural Wisdom, Artistic Perception, and Marginalised Voices. These are not exotic additions. They are the perspectives that determine whether "safety" means safety for everyone or safety for the people already in the room.

*The framework points toward the room. The room is not in the framework.*
